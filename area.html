The risk ratio describes the likelihood of a {{ title }} person being stopped and searched relative to the likelihood of a White person being stopped and searched.

If the risk ratio is 1, it means that the likelihood of being stopped is the same for the Black and White population.

If the risk ratio is greater than 1, it means that the likelihood of being stopped is greater for the Black population compared to White population. For example, a risk ratio of 2 would mean that the Black population are 2 times more likely to be stopped and searched than the White population.

If the risk ratio is less than 1, it means that the likelihood of being stopped is lower for the Black population compared to the White population. For example, a risk ratio of 0.50 would mean that the Black population are 50% less likely to be stopped and searched compared to the White population.

Because the risk ratio is an estimate, we cannot be 100% certain that the value is correct. However, we can estimate the range of values that the risk ratio is likely to be. This is what the bars on the graph represent (known as the 95% confidence interval). In essence, wider bars means less certainty in the risk ratio (because there is a wider range of possible values it could be), whilst narrower bars means more certainty in the risk ratio (because there is a narrower range of possible values it could be).
